Movement Analysis

This project analyzes home ranges and detected clusters of the spotted Nutcracker (Nucifraga caryocatactes) using GPS and sensor data from 115 tagged individuals.

Explore
01

Abstract

The spotted nutcracker (Nucifraga caryocatactes) serves as the primary seed disperser of Swiss stone pine (Pinus cembra), yet its spatial behaviour remains insufficiently studied.

GPS tracking data from 115 individuals were analysed to examine seasonal and individual variation in home range size, spatial overlap, and the potential of interactive tools for visualising movement ecology.

Home ranges were smallest during the breeding season and largest in autumn. Juvenile birds often showed extensive movements, indicating a role in long-distance seed dispersal and population connectivity.

Spatial overlap between individuals was common, particularly within the same valley. However, seasonal aggregation of data prevented conclusions about simultaneous space use.

An interactive RShiny application was developed to support exploration of movement patterns. Home ranges were estimated using 95% convex hulls, and DBSCAN clustering was applied to identify frequently visited areas.

The resulting tool facilitates interpretation of movement data and supports communication with researchers, decision-makers, and the wider public. Findings contribute to understanding seed dispersal, spatial connectivity, and potential responses to environmental change.

Introduction

The spotted Nutcracker (Nucifraga caryocatactes) is a highly specialized species of corvid, which can be found in coniferous mountain forests across Eurasia (BirdLife International, 2024). Through its mutually beneficial relationship with the Swiss stone pine (Pinus cembra), it plays a key ecological role as the tree’s primary seed disperser (Närmann et al., 2017; Graf et al., 2024). Each individual collects, moves and stores between 30’000 and 100’000 seeds annually (Närmann et al., 2017). Despite its important role, many aspects of the species ecology, in particular its breeding and habitat use, are still poorly understood (Rolando, 1996).

Previous studies suggest that the movement patterns of spotted nutcrackers are complex and shaped by the seasonal availability of resources and individual behavioral strategies (Graf et al., 2024; Rolando & Carisio, 1999). These individual differences in space use determine where seeds are cached and how likely they are to germinate. Of particular interest for seed distribution is the variability in individual movement behavior. While some nutcrackers maintain small, consistent home ranges, others show extensive and dynamic ranging patterns (Rolando & Carisio, 2001). Juveniles appear more exploratory and less site-faithful than adults, who reuse familiar caching territories.

Since the first use of GPS-tags, movement ecology has benefited greatly from the use of said technology to collect detailed data on animal movements. These advances allow fine-scale reconstruction of home ranges and frequently revisited sites, deepening our understanding of ecological behavior (Graf et al., 2024; Nathan et al., 2008).

Despite these tools and recent studies, we still lack a comprehensive understanding of how habitat use differs among individuals or over the year (Graf et al., 2024). This represents a critical knowledge gap, especially regarding consequences for population dynamics, gene flow and ecosystem functioning.

Additionally, recent work has raised concerns about the stability of the mutualistic relationship between nutcrackers and stone pines under climate change. As tree populations decline, nutcracker behaviour and survival may be directly affected — or help enable forest resilience through upslope colonisation (Engelhardt et al., 2020; Graf et al., 2024).

Our project aims to address key research gaps and support future studies. Specifically, we focus on: (1) how home ranges differ among individuals and seasons, (2) to what extent they overlap between individuals, and (3) how interactive web applications can help visualise these movement patterns.

    Preprocessing Overview
    Main Steps
    • Data cleaning: Removed missing coordinates and known faulty entries.
    • Date conversion: Standardised timestamps for temporal filtering.
    • Spatial conversion: Converted data into spatial features (using sf).
    • Season tagging: Assigned each point to a seasonal category (Breeding, Harvesting, Winter).
    • Track generation: Created movement lines per bird and month.
    • Clustering (DBSCAN): Identified frequently visited sites. DBSCAN was used for its ability to detect clusters without predefining their number and for robustness to noise. Parameters (eps, minPts) were adjusted manually based on movement scale and visual checks.
    • Export: Saved results as .geojson and .csv for direct use in the app.
    Additional Processing
    • Cluster review: Visual inspection to remove noise-related artefacts, important due to DBSCAN’s sensitivity.
    • Home ranges: Created convex hulls using 95% of points to estimate space use. This is robust but may still overestimate area if outliers remain.
    • Overlap matrix: Calculated spatial sharing between individuals. While it doesn’t prove simultaneous use, it highlights shared areas and possible connectivity.
    • Helper functions: Implemented for identifying overlaps and filtering relevant data efficiently.

    RShiny Development
    Overview

    This R Shiny app was developed to allow interactive exploration of bird tracking data. It offers two main views: a single-bird analysis and a comparison mode for two individuals. Users can apply seasonal filters, view spatial movement patterns, and access summary statistics and plots.

    The interface is built around the Shiny ui and server model. Data is loaded from GeoJSON and CSV files, preprocessed in earlier steps. Interactivity is implemented using reactivity and event-driven components. Users can switch between birds, toggle seasonal data, and see live updates on maps and charts.

    How the Code Works

    The app's logic is based on reactive programming. It uses conditional UI rendering, map proxies, and spatial operations to provide a responsive experience.

    • Bird and Season Selection: Bird IDs are loaded from tracking data. When the “multiple seasons only” filter is active, a function checks which individuals have data across two or more seasons (Breeding, Harvesting, Winter) and updates the selection list accordingly.
    • Navigation: Users can switch between birds using Previous/Next buttons. The current index is stored with reactiveVal. The list respects any filters that are active (e.g. multiple seasons).
    • Season Filter UI: Based on each bird’s available data, only valid seasons are shown as radio buttons. This is implemented using renderUI() and uiOutput() to keep the interface clean and relevant.
    • Home Range Calculation: The home range is calculated using a 95% Minimum Convex Polygon (MCP). First, the centroid of all points is determined, then distances from the centroid are computed. The furthest 5% are excluded, and a convex hull is created from the rest using st_convex_hull().
    • DBScan Clusters: Spatial clusters were computed during preprocessing and stored as polygons in GeoJSON format. The app filters and visualises these per bird and season. Area comparisons are also included as bar plots.
    • Map Rendering: All maps are rendered using leaflet. Map updates are handled with leafletProxy() to avoid full redraws. Layers for tracks, points, home ranges, and clusters are toggled via the control box.
    • Intersection Logic: When two birds are selected, their home ranges and clusters are intersected using st_intersection(). If an intersection exists, it is shown on the map and in the plots. Otherwise, a message appears instead of an empty result.
    • Heatmap: A simple heatmap view is provided in comparison mode. It merges points from both birds to visualise overall spatial intensity using leaflet.extras::addHeatmap().
    • Charts and Plots: Several plots are created using ggplot2: pie charts for seasonal data distribution, bar plots for home range sizes and cluster areas. All plots update dynamically based on current selection.

Discussion

© Nationalpark Stilfserjoch

The aim of this project was to explore and visualise the spatial behaviour of spotted nutcrackers across seasons. We focused on home range changes, individual overlaps, and the potential of interactive web tools for data exploration.

Based on GPS data from around 115 birds, we observed strong variation in home ranges across individuals and seasons. Breeding ranges were smallest, likely due to nesting, while movements expanded during harvesting. Juveniles often travelled farther – one even from Davos to Bormio and back – showing their role in long-distance seed dispersal.

Overlap between individuals was common, especially within the same valley. However, as data was grouped by season (not year), we couldn’t assess if areas were used simultaneously. Future work should combine season and year for better temporal resolution.

We estimated home ranges using 95% minimum convex polygons (MCPs). While efficient, MCPs can overestimate area. Our 95% filtering helped reduce this, but methods like KDE could better reflect intensity of space use.

DBSCAN clustering identified frequently visited sites without predefined cluster counts, but results depend on parameter choices. We adjusted values manually and checked outputs visually. More robust methods like HDBSCAN or auto-tuning could improve consistency.

The interactive RShiny app enables intuitive exploration of movement data and supports both research and public outreach. Features like year filtering, statistical layers, or live data integration could enhance future versions.

Main Findings

Home Range Variation

Sizes varied by individual and season: smallest during breeding, largest in autumn, especially for juveniles.

Juvenile Dispersal

Some juveniles moved long distances (e.g., Davos to Bormio), underlining their role in seed dispersal and connectivity.

Spatial Overlap

Home range Overlaps were frequent within valleys, though simultaneous use could not be confirmed due to seasonal grouping.

Interactive Dashboard

The RShiny app enables easy exploration of GPS data and supports both research and public outreach.

07

Conclusion

This project successfully explored the spatial ecology of spotted nutcrackers using GPS data from over 100 individuals. We identified strong seasonal variation in movement, with small home ranges during breeding and large-scale dispersal by juveniles in autumn.

Our interactive RShiny app allows users to filter and analyse movement patterns visually, bridging scientific research and public communication. However, some methodological limitations remain: the clustering method (DBSCAN) depends on parameter tuning, and MCP-based home ranges may not fully reflect actual space use. In addition, seasonal aggregation may mask year-to-year variation, limiting temporal resolution.

In future, integrating more advanced movement models and ecological theory — such as kernel density estimation (KDE), step selection analysis or temporal overlap metrics — could deepen insights. Still, our approach provides a solid and accessible foundation for applied movement research and communication.
